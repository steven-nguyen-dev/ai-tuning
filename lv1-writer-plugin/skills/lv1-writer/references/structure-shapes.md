# Structure shapes — how the piece is built

A piece can be true in every claim and still fall flat because of its *shape*. Two
shapes cover most non-fiction work; pick one in triage and record it in `00-triage.md`,
then `outline.md` builds to it. (Fiction follows its profile's own narrative
structure, not these.)

## Descriptive (horizontal)

Cut the topic into parts and describe each in turn. Sections are largely independent.

- **Good for:** overviews, market summaries, reference pieces, anything a reader will
  *skim*, PR or general-audience writing where readability beats argumentative force.
- **Strength:** clear, fast to read, easy to navigate.
- **Weakness:** no spine — a reader can't tell what the piece *argues*, and it's easy
  to bury a finding under flat description. This is the shape the first lv1-writer IT
  report used; it reads smoothly but carries less intellectual weight.

## Hypothesis-driven (vertical)

State one central thesis or mechanism early, then make every section apply it as a
lens, and close with a synthesis. The axiom shape.

- **Good for:** strategy, analysis, contested or high-stakes topics, anything meant to
  *convince* or to be *audited*.
- **The moves:**
  1. **Plant the mechanism** up front — one sentence the whole piece turns on.
  2. **Per-section lens** — open each section with the same question ("what does the
     mechanism do to X?") so sections compound instead of sitting side by side.
  3. **Synthesis** — a winners/losers or "what this means" section that pays off the
     mechanism, not a flat summary.
  4. **Steelman** when the conclusion is contested — assemble the strongest opposing
     case from real evidence, then show why the conclusion still stands or revise it.
- **Strength:** high argumentative force; a reader (or an inspector) can follow and
  test the logic end to end.
- **Weakness:** denser, higher cognitive load — wrong choice for a quick overview.

## Choosing

Default to **descriptive** for `tiny`/`normal` overviews and skimmable audiences;
choose **hypothesis-driven** for `full`/`high-stakes` analytical or persuasive work,
or whenever the task is to argue a position rather than survey a field. The
`analytical-intelligence`, `persuasive`, and (often) `cognitive-behavior` profiles
assume the hypothesis-driven shape. Record the choice and one line of why in
`00-triage.md`.
