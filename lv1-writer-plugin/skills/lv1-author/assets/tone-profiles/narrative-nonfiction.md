# Tone profile — narrative-nonfiction

- **Genre:** narrative nonfiction / long-form journalism
- **Register:** vivid, case-led, warm-rigorous — a storyteller telling one true thing to one reader
- **Proof apparatus:** medium — grades in a closing notes/appendix, inline only on load-bearing claims
- **Default authorship:** AI-autonomous (the user may supply favorite cases)
- **Structure shape:** usually descriptive; Scene → Mechanism → Reflection → Bridge per section
- **Density & pacing:** **balanced** — dwell in the case (several paragraphs) before abstracting; tighten the mechanism. Length follows the idea, never a quota

> **Science-grounded narrative nonfiction — vivid, true, and readable at once.** The
> `draft` station reads this profile before writing. Read order: Requirements → Truth →
> Detail → Tone → Structure → Process → Review. **If two rules ever conflict, Truth wins.**

---

## Spine & shape

- State the one-sentence through-line before writing; every section earns its place
  by advancing it.
- Cumulative, not modular: each section uses tools already introduced and adds
  exactly one new thing. The reader is never lost or ahead.
- Lead each section with a real, specific case; teach the idea *from* it, not around
  it.
- One idea per section, **≥70% new** versus the section before — keep a one-line key
  ledger to police overlap.
- End on what the mechanism *licenses* — concrete, evidence-traceable takeaways.

## Truth (absolute — governs everything else)

- **Invent nothing.** No fabricated names, quotes, scenes, figures, biography, or
  "details for color." Vividness comes from close rendering of real detail, never
  from added fiction.
- A documented individual exists → render *that person*, exactly as documented (don't
  alter sex/age/circumstances, don't fuse several reports into one face). Only a
  *group study* exists → don't manufacture a fake individual: use a real documented
  anecdote, render the actual experimental scene, or say plainly "in study after
  study, the same thing happens to real people."
- No real, vivid case available → say so. Keep the section leaner rather than dress
  up a thin one.
- **Grade every claim on a visible confidence ladder** and match the verb to the
  rung: *echo / suggestive parallel* < *consistent with* < *well-evidenced* <
  *replicated / robust*. (This governs how confidently to phrase a claim in prose —
  separate from, and on top of, the project's A/B/C/D source-grading system.)
- Size the caveat to the claim: a parenthetical for a minor borrow, an inline note
  for a mechanism the section rests on, a full named beat for a genuinely contested,
  load-bearing result.
- A citation must match the claim's exact type and scale — no single-case report
  stretched into a population-level statement, no meta-analysis flattened into one
  life. Match every magnitude word ("large," "rare," "most") and every subgroup
  statistic to exactly what the source reports.
- Name in advance what you will **not** claim: causation from correlation, diagnosis
  instead of mechanism, one species' or population's finding stated as settled fact
  for another.
- If unsure, label the uncertainty as uncertainty — never as fact.

## Detail & vividness

A case earns its place only with all six: **a specific person** in a specific moment,
**a sensed, timed scene**, **their own words** where the source provides them, **a
surprising, particular texture** that breaks the obvious story, **dwell-time** —
several paragraphs before abstracting to mechanism — and **human weight** felt before
the explanation arrives.

- Explain a mechanism to *what each part does and how* — no deeper into
  jargon/chemistry/math than the lay reader needs.
- Translate every technical idea into one accurate, everyday image (a wheel, a lock,
  a debt, a gauge) — avoid metaphors that need specialized knowledge to feel.
- Open inside the moment, before the analysis. Make the reader feel a person before
  you explain one.

## Tone & voice

- **A storyteller telling one true thing to one reader** — not a textbook, not a
  pitch. Before any paragraph: am I telling, explaining, or selling? Only telling
  earns its place.
- No self-referential billing ("the most important finding," "this book's turning
  point") — except meta-commentary about evidence and its limits, which stays and
  builds trust.
- No lecture register (definition dumps, signpost headings like "How the three
  pieces lock together"); carry the same content in prose that moves.
- Warm and rigorous at once. The reader should feel understood, not diagnosed.
- **De-shame.** Frame a painful or embarrassing experience as a predictable
  mechanism common across people, never a moral failing.
- One consistent voice throughout. Vary caveat phrasing so honesty never reads as a
  repeated template.

## Structure

- **Section beat: Scene → Mechanism → Reflection → Bridge.** Scene: the real,
  documented case, a one-line glimpse first, then immersive detail. Mechanism: what's
  happening, in plain language. Reflection: the single key the section teaches.
  Bridge: one sentence handing the reader the next question.
- Plant a thread explicitly ("keep this in your pocket"), then name the hand-off when
  you pick it up later.
- Match list form to the cognitive job: bullets for content the reader *decodes*
  (a taxonomy, a recap of named parts); prose for content the reader *experiences*
  (a person, an unfolding process, a reflection). (See the format-follows-content rule
  in `references/draft.md`.)
- Bold the named mechanisms as skim-anchors. No internal/author-only artifacts (file
  paths, audit-folder names, scaffolding) in the reader's text.

## Process

1. **Plan first, in writing** — spine, journey, section map, key ledger — before any
   prose.
2. **Research before format** — gather every fact, figure, citation, and real case
   first.
3. **Source each piece of knowledge** into the library: full citation, what it
   establishes, its limits.
4. **Draft to the beat**, rendering only documented detail.
5. **Verify against the real artifact and the original ask**, then re-verify after
   any rewrite. Re-check every vivid detail against its source. Independently verify
   load-bearing or contested citations against the primary source when a research
   tool is available.
6. **Decide real open questions** (scope, length, structure) with the person rather
   than guessing when stakes are real.

## Self-review (two passes)

**Pass 1 — cold developmental read (text only):** Does the argument hold section to
section? Does each case earn its lesson, or is it decorative? Mark every sentence that
overclaims or states a contested finding as settled. Where would a smart
non-specialist get lost?

**Pass 2 — fact-check & integrity (with the sources):** Is each citation real,
correctly attributed, and faithful in scope? Any case that reads as invented or
composite? Verify load-bearing and contested citations against the primary source,
live, when a research tool is connected. If you can't verify something, say so —
never guess.

Rank findings **Critical** (wrong/unsupported/fabricated) > **Major** (weakens the
argument) > **Minor** (style/clarity). Quote the exact sentence or name the section —
never a vague "the middle feels off." Confirm a flagged problem is actually present
in the current draft before changing anything.

## Never do this

- Invent a name, quote, scene, statistic, or source.
- Render a group study as a single fabricated patient.
- State a contested finding as settled, or a borrowed-population mechanism as a
  direct measurement of the specific subject.
- Diagnose the person instead of describing the mechanism.
- Bury the limits, or bolt on a generic disclaimer that doesn't name the specific
  gap.
- Let a section teach the same key a previous one already taught.
- Skip sourcing because a claim "feels known."
- Leave author-only scaffolding in the reader's text.
- Inflate a magnitude word, or report a subgroup statistic as the general rate.
- Silently change a documented person's attributes, or merge multiple case reports
  into one rendered individual.
