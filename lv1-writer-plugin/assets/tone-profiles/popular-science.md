# Tone profile — popular-science

- **Genre:** pure-science / popular-science book or long article
- **Register:** vivid, case-led, mechanism-explaining — curiosity-first, never a textbook
- **Proof apparatus:** full → medium, scaled by rigor tier (science is cited; grades in notes, inline on load-bearing claims)
- **Default authorship:** AI-autonomous (the user may supply favorite cases or framing)
- **Structure shape:** hook-case → mechanism → evidence → implication, per chapter
- **Density & pacing:** **balanced** — dwell on the hook case, then explain efficiently; don't rush the puzzle or pad the mechanism. Length follows the idea, never a quota

> Make a real reader *want* to understand something true and hard. The pleasure is the
> click of a mechanism falling into place — earned with real evidence, never with a
> tidy story that the data doesn't support. Read order: Requirements → Truth → Detail →
> Tone → Structure → Review. **If two rules conflict, Truth wins.**

---

## Spine & shape

- One **driving question** per book/chapter, stated early; every section moves toward
  answering it.
- Per-section beat: **hook (a concrete case, experiment, or puzzle) → mechanism (how
  it actually works) → evidence (how we know, and how sure) → implication (what it
  licenses)**.
- Cumulative: each chapter uses ideas already built and adds exactly one. Introduce
  a concept before relying on it.
- Build an **evidence ladder** — distinguish a suggestive single study from a
  replicated, robust result, and phrase confidence to match.

## Truth (absolute)

- **Invent nothing** — no fabricated experiments, scientists, figures, or "for-color"
  detail. Render only documented cases and real studies.
- **Match the claim to the science:** correlation is not causation; an animal or
  in-vitro result is not a human clinical fact; a single study is not consensus. Say
  which you have.
- **Grade load-bearing and contested claims** (A/B/C/D against the source library);
  show the working for any number or effect size. Don't inflate magnitude words
  ("huge", "proven", "most").
- Where the science is genuinely **unsettled or debated**, say so at the point of use —
  that honesty is part of the appeal, not a weakness.
- Translate jargon into one accurate everyday image, but never let the metaphor smuggle
  in a claim the science doesn't make.

## Detail & vividness

- Open inside a real moment — a researcher at a bench, a patient, a field
  observation — before abstracting to the principle.
- Explain a mechanism to *what each part does and how*, no deeper into the math/chemistry
  than a curious layperson needs.
- Dwell on the concrete case before the generalization; let the reader feel the puzzle
  before the resolution.

## Tone & voice

- Warm, curious, precise. A knowledgeable friend explaining something they love — not
  a lecturer, not a hype-merchant.
- No self-billing ("the most astonishing discovery in science"); let the real result
  impress on its own.
- Meta-commentary about evidence and its limits stays — it builds trust.

## Structure & format

- Chapter: driving question → hook case → mechanism → evidence ladder → implication →
  bridge to the next question.
- **Format follows content** (see `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/references/draft.md`): prose for the unfolding
  story and mechanism; a table when comparing several studies, organisms, or numbers;
  dashes/arrows for a pathway or sequence; bullets only for a genuine list (e.g. named
  parts to recap). Default to prose; reach for markup when it genuinely clarifies.
- Bold named mechanisms as skim-anchors. No author-only scaffolding in the reader's text.

## Process

1. Plan the question, the journey, and the section map in writing first.
2. Research the real studies and cases before drafting; source each into the library
   with its limits.
3. Draft to the beat, rendering only documented detail.
4. Verify every figure, effect size, and study description against its source; re-check
   after any rewrite. Verify contested/load-bearing claims against the primary source.

## Self-review (two passes)

**Pass 1 (text only):** Does each chapter answer its driving question? Does each case
earn its lesson? Mark every sentence that overstates certainty or flattens a debated
result into settled fact.

**Pass 2 (with sources):** Is every study real, correctly described, faithful in
scope? Any effect size inflated, any animal/lab result stated as human fact? Verify
load-bearing claims against primaries. If you can't verify, say so.

## Never do this

- Invent a study, a scientist, a number, or a vivid detail.
- State correlation as causation, or a single/animal/lab finding as settled human fact.
- Inflate an effect size or a magnitude word beyond the source.
- Let a metaphor carry a claim the evidence doesn't.
- Bury the uncertainty or bolt on a generic disclaimer instead of naming the specific gap.
