# Tone profile — academic

- **Genre:** academic paper / literature review
- **Register:** measured, formal, citation-dense — precise and impersonal
- **Proof apparatus:** **full**, in academic citation style (inline citations + reference list; A/B/C/D maps to evidence strength)
- **Default authorship:** AI-autonomous
- **Structure shape:** discipline-conventional (e.g. IMRaD for empirical; thematic for a review)
- **Density & pacing:** **compress** — shortest rigorous path to the claim; tables/figures over prose for data; no rhetorical padding

> Contribute a precise, well-evidenced argument to a scholarly conversation. Every
> claim is located in the literature; nothing is asserted beyond what the evidence and
> the method support. Read order: Requirements → Truth → Rigor → Structure → Review.
> **If two rules conflict, Truth wins.**

---

## Spine & shape

- State the **research question / thesis and the gap** it addresses early; every
  section serves it.
- Follow the field's convention: **IMRaD** (Introduction → Methods → Results →
  Discussion) for empirical work; a structured thematic synthesis for a literature
  review; clear premises→argument→objections→conclusion for a theoretical piece.
- Situate the contribution: what is known, what is missing, what this adds.

## Truth & rigor

- **Every claim is cited** to a real, correctly attributed source (author, year,
  venue). A source supports a claim only if it actually establishes *that* claim — not
  topical adjacency.
- **Never fabricate** a citation, a quotation, a statistic, or a result. This is the
  cardinal academic offence.
- **Scope precisely:** a subgroup finding is not a population finding; a single study
  is not consensus; a correlational result is not causal. Preserve every magnitude and
  qualifier exactly as the source reports.
- **Grade evidence strength** (A/B/C/D underneath the citation style): distinguish
  replicated/robust from single-study or contested, and phrase accordingly. `[A]` requires
  the source **present in this session**, not recalled from training — a citation you
  cannot point to a fetched source for is not yet an `[A]`.
- **Engage the strongest counter-position** (steelman) — represent opposing scholarship
  fairly, then argue, rather than ignoring or strawmanning it.
- State **limitations** honestly; don't bury them.

## Tone & voice

- Formal, measured, impersonal where the field expects it; precise vocabulary used
  consistently (define terms at first use).
- No rhetoric inflated beyond the evidence; hedge exactly to the strength of support —
  neither over-claiming nor false modesty.

## Structure & format

- Section structure per convention; numbered sections if the venue uses them.
- **Format follows content** (see `${CLAUDE_PLUGIN_ROOT}/references/draft.md`): tables/figures for data
  (numbered, captioned, self-contained, referenced in text); prose for argument;
  equations/notation where the field requires. Every figure cited in text exists and
  vice versa.
- Full reference list; every in-text citation appears in it and vice versa (no orphans).

## Process

1. Define the question and locate the literature first; build the source library.
2. Outline to the field's structure; map which sources support which sections.
3. Draft with citations as you go; show methods/derivations reproducibly.
4. Verify every citation against its source for existence, attribution, and scope;
   check every cross-reference resolves.

## Never do this

- Fabricate or misattribute a source, quote, or result.
- Cite a topically-related source that doesn't establish the specific claim.
- Overstate scope (subgroup→population, single study→consensus, correlation→cause).
- Ignore or strawman the strongest opposing scholarship.
- Leave orphan citations or unresolved cross-references.
