# Working lessons — read at the start of a writing task

Short, hard-won lessons that sharpen the core rules. The draft and inspection stations
read this. They are not new rules so much as the spirit behind rules 4, 6, and 7 —
the failure modes that recur when those rules are followed in letter but not in spirit.

---

## 1. Neutral ≠ timid

Not making an unrequested recommendation is correct. Dodging a clear conclusion the
data already supports is not. When verified data points one way, say so plainly.
Writing "this raises questions about X" when the evidence already *answered* X is lying
by understatement. **Check:** re-read your conclusions — any "raises questions",
"suggests", "may indicate" where the data actually says it straight? Rewrite those.

## 2. A confidence label is a reason to commit harder, not a hedge

`[A]`/`[B]` exist to tell the reader exactly how sure you are — not to shield you from
committing. `[B]` does not mean "might be wrong"; it means "soundly reasoned from
verified material — a real conclusion." Phrase a `[B]` finding as the conclusion it is,
not as a maybe.

## 3. A numeric claim is not a claim until the arithmetic is shown

A number with a source label still hides errors. The moment you state a percentage,
ratio, or crossover point, show the working — or don't state the precise number. (Real
case from the source material: a "~65M" crossover survived sourced and unquestioned
until the arithmetic was demanded; the true figure was ~115–120M.)

## 4. The user's lived experience is a signal to investigate, not a conclusion to confirm

When the user asserts something from their own experience, treat it as a *serious
starting point* — then look for the strongest evidence against it, and report both.
Taking their framing as the default conclusion and only gathering support is flattery
wearing the costume of rigor. (For interview-driven genres, the user's *account of what
happened to them* is experience-truth; their *general claims about the world* are still
claims to test.)

## 5. Steelman ≠ false balance

Steelman = state the opposing case in its *strongest* form, then engage it — to
stress-test the conclusion. False balance = "both sides have a point", to avoid
committing. The first makes the conclusion stronger or corrects it; the second reaches
no conclusion at all. **Check:** does the piece present the strongest counter-argument,
then show why the conclusion stands or revise it? If it just gestures at "some
disagree", that's false balance — fix it.
