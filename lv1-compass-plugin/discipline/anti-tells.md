# Anti-tells — the AI register to write *out* of

Read this before drafting; the inspector checks against it (register conformance, check 6).
These are the words and constructions that mark prose as machine-generated. They are not
banned from the dictionary — most have an honest literal use. They are banned as
**reflexive filler**: the word the model reaches for when it isn't saying anything.

The rule: **a literal, earned use is fine; a reflexive, decorative one is a FIX-IT.** "The
vendor's pricing landscape shifted after the acquisition" — if you mean the actual market of
prices, fine. "Navigating the complexities of the modern data landscape" — a tell. When in
doubt, cut the word and see if the sentence lost any meaning — if not, it was a tell.

## Generic-AI tells

`delve`, `tapestry`, `testament` (as in "a testament to"), `navigate the complexities`,
`it's worth noting that`, `it's important to note`, `in today's fast-paced world`, `in the
realm of`, `landscape` (figurative — "the funding landscape"), `supercharge`, `unlock` (your
potential / insights), `beacon`, `realm`, `embark on`, `at the end of the day`, `when it
comes to`, `the world of`, `a rich tapestry of`, `ever-evolving`, `multifaceted`,
`underscores`, `boasts` (a feature).

## Hype tells (persuasive / marketing drift)

`game-changer`, `revolutionary`, `seamless`, `cutting-edge`, `next-level`, `transformative`
(unless you show the transformation), `unprecedented` (unless it literally is and you say
why), `paradigm shift`, `robust` (as an empty intensifier), `best-in-class`, `world-class`.
In a work register these read as unearned claims — say the specific thing the adjective is
standing in for, or cut it.

## Structural tells

- **The empty pivot:** "But here's the thing…", "Here's the kicker…", "And that's the
  beauty of it." Earn the turn with content, don't announce it.
- **The triad reflex:** forcing every idea into a rule-of-three list ("clearer, faster,
  stronger") when the content has two points or four.
- **The summary echo:** restating the paragraph you just wrote as its own closing sentence.
- **Throat-clearing openers:** "In order to understand X, we must first…", "Let's dive in."
- **Reflexive hedging:** stacking "it could be argued that", "somewhat", "arguably",
  "in some sense" around a claim the evidence actually supports. In a work register this is
  the opposite failure of hype — a label is a reason to commit, not to soften (neutral ≠
  timid; see `working-lessons.md`).

## How the inspector applies this

Register conformance (`agents/lv1-inspect.md`, check 6) flags **a cluster of these tells**
as a FIX-IT — not a single incidental literal use. The target is the *register*, not one
word. A brief that reads like marketing copy, or an analysis buried in reflexive hedging,
fails even if no single phrase is individually damning. Both work registers
(analytical · neutral-professional) are judged against this list.
